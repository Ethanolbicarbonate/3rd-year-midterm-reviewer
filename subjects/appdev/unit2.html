Unit 2: Emerging Technologies

Introduction to Emerging Technologies

Emerging technologies are novel, rapidly evolving, and potentially disruptive innovations in their early stages of development or adoption. They represent advancements that could significantly alter industries and societies but are not yet fully mature or widely implemented.

Their role is to act as catalysts for groundbreaking innovation, enabling new business models, optimizing operations, and creating disruptive products and services that shape the future of applications and human life.

Technology Adoption Lifecycle

The technology adoption lifecycle explains how new technologies spread through society over time, from the initial risk-takers to the final skeptics.

Innovators 2.5%

Adventurous risk-takers who love trying brand-new, experimental tech and provide early feedback.

Early Adopters 13.5%

Visionaries who spot potential early and use technology to gain an advantage. Their success stories convince others.

Early Majority 34%

Practical users who adopt technology once it's proven to be stable, reliable, and well-supported.

Late Majority 34%

A cautious group that adopts only when the technology is widespread, affordable, and easy to use.

Laggards 16%

Skeptics who avoid change and adopt a technology only when there are no alternatives or it becomes necessary.

Artificial Intelligence (AI) & Machine Learning (ML)

The core purpose of AI and ML is to create machines capable of replicating and mimicking human intelligence, enabling them to perform tasks that normally require human intervention like independent learning, complex reasoning, and logical decision-making.

Use Cases in Application Development

Chatbots

Software that uses AI and Natural Language Processing (NLP) to mimic human conversation. Used to automate customer interaction and provide 24/7 support.

Recommendation Systems

Algorithms that analyze user behavior and preferences to suggest relevant content, like on Netflix or Amazon.

Image Classification

An AI technique trained to recognize and categorize images, helping computers "see" and understand visual data for tasks like photo tagging.

Tools for AI Development

  • TensorFlow: An open-source platform for ML using data flow graphs.
  • PyTorch: An open-source deep learning framework known for its flexibility and ease of use with Python.
  • Azure AI: Microsoft's cloud-based offering with a set of AI tools and services.

Ethical Concerns

Bias & Discrimination: AI can inherit or amplify unfair biases from its training data, leading to discriminatory outcomes.
Privacy Risks: Large-scale data collection for training AI models can lead to surveillance, data misuse, and breaches of personal information.

Internet of Things (IoT)

The Internet of Things (IoT) refers to a network of physical objects ("things") embedded with sensors, software, and other technologies that allow them to connect and exchange data over the Internet. It is essentially an extension of the Internet into the physical world.

Its purpose is to connect physical objects to the digital world, enabling them to collect and share data. This allows for remote monitoring, automation, and smarter decision-making.

Applications and Integration

Smart Homes

Connected devices like thermostats, lights, and security cameras that can be controlled remotely via mobile or web apps.

Wearables

Devices like smart fitness trackers and smartwatches that monitor health and activity, syncing data to a user's smartphone app.

Augmented Reality (AR) & Virtual Reality (VR)

Both AR and VR alter human perception through digital technology, moving computing from a 2D screen to an intuitive, 3D space. They are converging under the umbrella of Extended Reality (XR).

  • Augmented Reality (AR) enhances the real world by overlaying digital information onto it. It focuses on improving real-world experiences, accessible via smartphones and tablets.
  • Virtual Reality (VR) completely replaces a user's real-world environment with a computer-generated simulation, providing full immersion with special headsets and controllers.

Use Cases in Applications

Education

AR apps can bring textbook diagrams to life, while VR provides immersive training simulations for complex tasks (e.g., surgery).

Retail

AR allows customers to virtually "try on" makeup or see how furniture fits in their home (e.g., IKEA Place app).

Gaming

AR games like Pok√©mon Go overlay gameplay onto the real world, while VR offers deeply immersive gaming experiences.

Blockchain & Decentralized Applications (dApps)

Blockchain is a distributed, decentralized, and tamper-proof digital ledger that securely records transactions. Its decentralized nature means it isn't dependent on a single authority, making it highly resilient.

Basics and Real-World Uses

Smart Contracts & dApps

Smart Contracts are self-executing contracts with the terms of the agreement directly written into code. Decentralized Applications (dApps) run on a blockchain or peer-to-peer network instead of a single computer.

Identity Verification

Provides a secure and decentralized way to manage and verify digital identities without relying on a central authority.

Supply Chain

Offers transparent and immutable tracking of goods from origin to destination, reducing fraud and improving efficiency.

Digital Currency

The foundation for cryptocurrencies like Bitcoin, enabling peer-to-peer electronic cash systems without a central bank.

Summary Points

  • Emerging technologies are new, rapidly evolving innovations with the potential to be disruptive.
  • AI aims to mimic human intelligence for tasks like chatbots and recommendation systems, but carries ethical risks like bias and privacy concerns.
  • IoT connects physical objects to the internet, enabling smart devices like home assistants and wearables.
  • AR overlays digital info onto the real world, while VR creates a fully immersive digital world.
  • Blockchain is a decentralized ledger technology that enables secure and transparent systems like smart contracts and digital currencies.
